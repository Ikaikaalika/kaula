# Research Statement

## Tyler Gee  
**Prospective research area:** Efficient multimodal world models, generative predictive learning, state-space dynamics, and hardware-aware machine learning systems

My long-term research goal is to build learning systems that do more than classify or imitate. I want to develop models that form internal representations of how the world evolves, use those representations to predict plausible futures, and eventually support reasoning, planning, and autonomous decision-making. The line of work that most excites me is the intersection of **world models**, **multimodal representation learning**, **efficient sequence modeling**, and **generative uncertainty modeling**.

My current proposed research project, **Efficient World Modeling with Joint Embeddings, State-Space Dynamics, and Latent Diffusion**, reflects this direction. The project is motivated by a core limitation of many modern sequence models: transformers are powerful, but their memory and compute costs can make long-horizon predictive modeling expensive. At the same time, deterministic next-state predictors often fail in environments where multiple futures are possible. My proposed approach combines a **joint embedding space** for observations, actions, and optional language; a **state-space dynamics core** for efficient temporal prediction; and a **latent diffusion head** for representing stochastic future trajectories. The model is designed first as an Apple-silicon-native MLX prototype and then as a system that can scale to larger GPU clusters.

This project matters to me for two reasons. First, I am deeply interested in models that move beyond token prediction and toward **predictive understanding**. A world model that can represent compact latent states, evolve them over time, and imagine multiple futures is a step toward more grounded intelligence. Second, I care about **systems efficiency**. I do not see efficiency as a secondary engineering concern; I see it as a scientific constraint that shapes which ideas are practical to test and scale. This is why I am interested in state-space models and hardware-aware implementations, including Apple Silicon, Metal, and eventually larger distributed systems.

The central research question of my project is whether a joint-embedding state-space world model with latent diffusion can achieve competitive predictive performance while improving efficiency relative to transformer-based baselines. I plan to test this through staged experiments: first on synthetic temporal data to validate the full learning pipeline, then on a real video dataset such as EPIC-KITCHENS or Ego4D, and finally on an action-conditioned dataset such as DROID or BridgeData V2. My evaluation will focus not only on predictive quality, but also on rollout stability, memory usage, throughput, and the effect of adding stochastic latent modeling through diffusion.

Methodologically, I am especially interested in modular but principled design. In this project, the encoder maps observations into a shared latent space; the state-space dynamics model evolves latent states forward in time; the diffusion head models uncertainty over future latents; and the decoder reconstructs future observations or targets. The training objective combines latent prediction loss, reconstruction loss, diffusion denoising loss, and an optional alignment loss for multimodal consistency. This structure allows for meaningful ablations: deterministic versus stochastic prediction, SSM versus transformer dynamics, and unimodal versus multimodal latent spaces.

I see this project as the beginning of a larger research agenda. In the near term, I want to answer focused empirical questions about the tradeoff between efficiency and predictive quality. In the longer term, I want to explore how world models can support planning, reinforcement learning, multimodal reasoning, and agentic behavior. I am particularly drawn to research environments that value both scientific depth and implementation rigor, where it is possible to move fluidly between mathematical modeling, algorithm design, and systems-level execution.

My background has shaped this perspective. I have broad technical interests spanning machine learning, quantitative reasoning, chemical engineering, and applied AI systems. Across projects, I am most motivated by problems that require connecting theory to working systems. I enjoy building from scratch, comparing alternatives carefully, and treating failures as informative rather than discouraging. Graduate study is the right next step for me because I want to deepen my mathematical and scientific foundations while contributing to ambitious research problems in predictive intelligence.

In graduate school, I hope to work with researchers who care about one or more of the following questions: How should efficient temporal architectures be designed for long-horizon predictive learning? What representations best support multimodal world models? How should uncertainty be modeled in latent predictive systems? And how can hardware constraints be treated not as limitations, but as design signals for new model families? My goal is to become the kind of researcher who can both propose new model ideas and build the systems needed to test them convincingly.

Ultimately, I want my research to help close the gap between pattern recognition and genuine predictive modeling. I believe world models are one of the most promising routes toward that goal, and I am excited to pursue that direction through rigorous graduate study.
